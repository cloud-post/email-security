Learn What DMARC Is And Why It's Essential For Your Business

DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance) is an email authentication protocol that is designed to protect a business from email-based phishing and spoofing attacks. It works by sending reports from service providers such as Gmail or Yahoo to a designated “DMARC record” that is associated with the domain. 

It allows businesses to take action to protect their domain from malicious emails by providing guidelines on how to best handle unauthenticated emails and how to respond to suspicious activity. What Is DMARC?



DMARC, or Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance, is an email authentication protocol that works to ensure email messages are sent from a legitimate source and can be trusted by the recipient. DMARC is designed to detect and prevent spoofed emails from reaching an inbox, thereby reducing the likelihood of malicious messages from reaching the intended recipient.

DMARC works through the use of Sender Policy Framework (SPF) and DomainKeys Identified Mail (DKIM) authentication protocols, which are both used to verify that an email was sent from the domain it claims to be sent from. 

DMARC also enables email senders to receive reports on the messages being sent and provides feedback on how to improve email deliverability. By using DMARC, organizations can ensure their emails are trusted and reduce the risk of spoofing.

How Does DMARC Work?


DMARC uses two existing email authentication protocols, SPF (Sender Policy Framework) and DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail), to provide domain owners with greater control over their email domain. DMARC checks the alignment of the SPF and DKIM records against the domain in the message’s “From” header. If the records match, the email is considered to have passed DMARC authentication.

DMARC also provides reporting functionality, allowing domain owners to receive feedback on how their email domain is being used. This feedback can help businesses identify potential issues with their email authentication and take steps to address them.
