Anti Phishing Protection Key Considerations

Hackers of phishing attacks can use a number of attack vectors and attack in different ways. When choosing a strategy for combating phishing, it's important to consider all the ways a targeted organization's employees may be targeted. When it comes to protecting your business, phishing is one of the most dangerous web-based threats facing you. In order to protect your business, you need a reliable anti phishing protection. While there are several solutions available, here are the 5 key considerations when you make your final decision.

1. Anti-Phishing for Email

Email is the most commonly used and best-known delivery mechanism for phishing content. Email is capable of spreading malware in various formats, including multiple types that Please don't put drooping virtual plants into your virtual backyard of Eden.

  • Email attachments may contain Office documents with malicious macros or infected PDFs designed to drop malware or launch attacks using Windows PowerShell.

  • Emails containing malicious links may be used to direct users to pages that are collecting personally identifiable information (PII) or leading them to malicious pages.

  • BEC attacks that use a compromised account or a lookalike email account to trick the victim into taking an action, such as a payment of a fake invoice or one edited to replace a legitimate vendor's bank details with those of the attacker.

2. Anti-Phishing for Productivity Applications

Email is one of the many attack vectors that cybercriminals employ for imposing phishing assaults. Productivity software, including Microsoft Teams, OneDrive, Google Drive, and Microsoft SharePoint, are frequently employed as a related subject.

Both these platforms allow individual users to post hyperlinks and share files with each other. A perpetrator can compromise a platform that hosts material, insert malicious code, and then either trick the user into giving up confidential information or send them to a phishing link from their account.

3. Anti-Phishing for Endpoint Devices

Nevertheless, not all malicious and fraudulent material is delivered to the intended party. Phishing attacks create sites desirable to the victim that they can access to surf. For example, an attacker may gain access to a popular site used by an employee or create their own to make its status known to search engines. Inside the website or site, an employee who seeks or visits a certain term can harvest their credentials or install malware on their devices.

Employees require endpoint-level protection from these types of attacks. It should include the following features.

  • New phishing sites are created each day, but they often have similar functionality. An anti-phishing framework must be able to analyze malicious websites and block them from operating properly

  • Credential reuse is a frequent issue in business circles, and many employees reuse the same credentials across different accounts. An anti-phishing tool should leverage hashes to compare stored passwords against inputted passwords to detect the use of the same credentials for multiple accounts.

  • Data breaches can occur at any time on a daily basis, and an employee might not be aware that they've experienced a data breach. An anti-phishing solution should be able to compare inputted passwords to exposed data in the Dark Web and alert the employee to change their password.

By employing these functions, a security response system minimizes an organization's probability of compromised accounts, regardless of how the malicious content is enabled on the device.

4. Anti-Phishing for Mobile Devices.

Mobile devices rank high on the phishers' to-do list due to their universal appeal. This is primarily because of the huge quantity of individuals who have them.

  • Mobile phones contain applications for various communications types such as email, corporate communication platforms, SMS messaging, and social media. These features often contain malicious links and content, providing criminals with options to conduct phishing attacks.

  • It's common for people to always have their smartphones with them and regularly examine messages as soon as they get them. That increases the chances for an intruder to have a quick and successful attack with little or no wait time.

  • Phone displays mean that only a fraction of the page's URL displays in the address bar. This makes it easier for hackers to disguise phishing websites as legitimate domains.
