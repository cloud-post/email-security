How To Prevent Phishing Before It Strikes


In recent years, scammers have increasingly turned to email and text messages as a way to target victims. By impersonating a trusted source, scammers can trick victims into revealing sensitive information like passwords, account numbers, or Social Security numbers. This information can then be used to gain access to important accounts or be sold to other criminals.

Despite the risk, many people still fall victim to these scams. That's why it's important to be aware of the different techniques scammers use and to know how to protect yourself. For additional information, please visit this page.

If you receive an unsolicited email or text message asking for personal information, don't respond. And if you're ever unsure about the authenticity of a message, contact the supposed sender directly to verify. By taking these precautions, you can help keep your information safe and avoid becoming a victim of fraud.



Scammers hone their strategies as new developments happen or trends evolve in phishing emails and text messages.

You might get an unexpected email or text message that appears to emanate from a business or group you are familiar with, such as your bank or a credit card company. It might originate with an online payment portal. The message may pertain specifically to your finances or your knowledge of the sender.

  • They say they've seen some suspicious activity or log-in attempts they haven't claimed there's an issue with your account or your payment information there isn't say there is some personal and financial information you aren't familiar with it isn't authentic wants you to click on a link to make a payment but the link has malware.

  • There's no call for the government to forgive you to help you.

  • It's a scam to say you are eligible for a refund when it offers a coupon for free stuff.

Consider what it would be like to be fooled by an email like this. Recipients of this message are generally deceived into thinking that it's genuine. 

If you have seen the email and it supposedly indicates a relationship with a business you know, but the style, layout, and content don’t align with how the company normally operates, it's likely that the email is extortion.

  • The greeting in the email is generic.

  • In the email, it states that your account is put on hold because of some billing issue. 

  • The email invites you to click on a link so you can update your payment details.

While real companies might correspond with you by email, legitimate companies won t email or text through a link to update your payment information. Phishing emails can generally cause problems for those who make fraudulent accounts or give scammers their information, resulting in identity theft. And their reputation can be damaged whenever they spoof big-name businesses.


How To Protect Yourself From Phishing Attacks




Hackers are always attempting to outsmart your email spam filters, so adding extra layers of defense will help you. Here are four ways to protect yourself from phishing attacks.


Four Ways To Protect Yourself From Phishing


  • Be sure to protect your computer using security software. Set the security software program to update automatically to deal with any new security threats.

  • Protect your cell phone by creating software to update automatically. These updates may offer the high level of security you need to defend yourself against security threats.

  • Back up your data to the best of your ability. Back up the data on your laptop to an external drive or in the cloud. Back up the data on your cell phone as well.

Overall, "Phishing" is a type of con game in which people try to obtain sensitive information such as credit card numbers, passwords, and bank account information by masquerading as a trustworthy entity in electronic communication. Users should be very careful when clicking links from unknown or untrusted sources and never enter personal information.
