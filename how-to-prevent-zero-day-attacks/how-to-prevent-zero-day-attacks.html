How to Prevent Zero Day Attacks

To help protect your computer from being hacked into, it's a good idea to use antivirus software that can keep the bad guys at bay. Unfortunately, you can't put a "lock" on your operating system and software. These Systems are always vulnerable to hackers, but there are ways to prevent your computer from getting struck in a zero day attack.

What is a Zero-Day Attack?

A zero-day attack, or otherwise known as a computer security vulnerability which hackers exploit, occurs after it has been discovered but not fixed by developers as quickly as possible. The cyberattack is chiefly motivated by profit, in which cybercriminals illegally access and sell sensitive data.To be accepted in the hacking community, notoriety plays an important role in the second.

Zero-day attacks can materialize on a variety of software systems. This covers browsers, operating systems, office applications, as well as the Internet of Things (IoT). In addition to this, they can also affect physical devices by targeting hardware like wireless routers.

What Risks Do Zero-Day Attacks Pose?

Cybercriminals are capable of exploiting zero-day exploits to obtain entrance to your network. Once they take advantage of this access, they can devote an increased amount of time modifying your network's configuration to spread malware. This can allow hackers to gain entry to the rest of your network, causing major monetary losses.

How To Prevent Zero Day Attacks

Safeguard your team against zero-day risks by implementing certain measures

Inform Your Employees About Cybersecurity Risks

As is the case with a cyberattack, it's crucial that employees take all precautions to prevent them. Instruction will need to be provided on modern threats as well as how to prevent new ones. Long-term cybersecurity training is also essential to maintain that alert staff and prevent them from becoming outdated.

Regularly Check for Patches and Bug Fixes 

This may seem like a double-edged sword, but it is a necessity regardless of how harmful new updates might be. Yes, new updates sometimes pave the way for threats but also have a significant role to play. As such, you should install new updates as soon as possible to limit risk of an attack.

Secure Every New Device 

Before installing new hardware that works with your current systems, ensure that the latest security software is installed on your computers and other devices. This includes desktop computers, laptops, mobile phones, and any other hardware that connects to a wider network.

Implement Behavior-Based Detection Systems

Behavior-based detection systems enable us to identify the intent of an action within a software program. The system then checks to find out if the action was intentional or aimed at a change in function. In case the system notices a deliberate change in the functions it monitors, it will sound the alarms.

Behavior-based detection systems operate more precisely than traditional threat detection systems because they take in to consideration the intents of an action before it has already been completed. Modern threat detection models often fail to identify proven zero-day vulnerabilities because they are not yet well understood by the business.

By applying such systems to your firm, you can lower the risk of zero-day attacks exponentially.

Do Not Install Unnecessary Software Programs

With every new software system your firm implements onto your computers, there will always be new vulnerabilities. You should only install software that's necessary for your business operations. Otherwise, it's best to remove the program in order to reduce the potential of further threats to your security.

Work with Trusted Partners 

Presumably, your company makes use of a variety of partners. A partner that doesn't offer the same security standards as your organization may be responsible for infections that enter your system.

An effective defense includes real-time monitoring of all devices on the network, and checking what applications are being used. Security vendors are responding by limiting the number of applications that can be run on the device at one time. Additionally, alerts are created when users attempt to run new applications, and these alerts are presented to the user.
