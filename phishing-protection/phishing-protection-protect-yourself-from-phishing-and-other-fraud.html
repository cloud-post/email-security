Phishing Protection:
Protect Yourself From Phishing And Other Fraud


In the online world, nothing is more frustrating than being tricked into reconfiguring your email account and losing access to your important messages. It's frustrating enough when you're the one being tricked into changing your email settings, but it's downright infuriating when you realize someone else has been impersonating you and changing your email account's password. Luckily, there are various steps you can take to protect you from phishing and other fraud.

Cyberattacks carried out by email are often referred to as phishing. Hackers design emails to attack the target and make them divulge personal information, or to trick them into downloading malware. Hackers may attempt to defraud your bank account and credit card issuer, or your birthdate and social security number.

Phishing email scams work by attempting to shock, threaten, or trick you into responding without thinking. Watch out for sentences with these or similar words.

  • You will be required to click a link to cancel your account.
  • Thank you for your payment of $2,374.27. Click here for more information.
  • We have detected unusual activity on your account. Click here to verify it or access will be terminated.
  • To qualify for this special offer, reply to this message with your email address, password, and date of birth.

Phishing scams might ask you to confirm personal information by responding to a request or going to a fake website that resembles a legitimate one. For instance, a phisher may create a fake Gmail, Facebook, or University Web page to trick you into "logging into" your account with their real username and password.

Providing personal information in an incorrect response email or via a phony site jeopardizes your accounts as well as your identity. Hackers might use your accounts to steal money or place an identity theft, and they can use your personal information to commit the crime.

Some guidelines to remember:

  • If you are uncertain about the authenticity of an email you have received, attempt to reach the sender by phone or address.
  • Examine the links before you click the email. For example, if the email supposedly came from the University, ensure that the links go to a legitimate "udel.edu" site.
  • Watch out for messages with a large number of errors, as they tend to be scams.
  • Be wary of any contact that asks you for personal information by clicking a link.
  • Do not open unsolicited or suspicious email attachments.
  • Don't include your private Social Security number, credit card information, banking credentials, or passwords in any private email messages.
  • Unless the message is sent by someone you know, you should never reply with personal information in unsolicited electronic messages, text messages, social media, or e-mails.

Tips for avoiding phishing scams

Here are the some tips to avoid phishing scams:

Don't believe everything you read:

Phishing predators generally rely on fear and urgency to trigger their victims into responding.

Check links carefully: 

Hover over the hyperlink and review for proper abbreviations or suspicious websites.

Don't open unexpected attachment:

Phishing attacks utilize malicious attachments to execute scripts and compromise your computer.

Never give up personal information: 

Legitimate organizations will not ask you to provide any personal information via email.

Go to the source: 

Confirm the trustworthiness of a message by contacting the supposed sender indirectly (e.g., over the phone or in person). If you're directed to a page for something, verify that the website is legitimate.

In conclusion, let's summarize steps that we can all take to fight back against these crooks. First, we need to be careful when placing our trust in people. And second, we need to realize that there are real, legitimate opportunities for making money online and that scammers aren't going to stop them.
